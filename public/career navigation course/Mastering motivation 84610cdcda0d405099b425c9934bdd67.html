Mastering motivation

You will have moments where the motivation is high, you have the wind at your back, the sun is out, you are making great progress, you’re feeling great.

You will have moments where there is no motivation, the wind is blowing in your face, it’s pouring rain, you are moving backwards, you’re feeling hopeless.

The latter are the moments that define your journey. They are the moments that cause you to quit if you aren’t focused on the larger goal.

Why I wanted the job at Amazon

I’ll give a personal anecdote. It was 2020. I was already 7 years into my career, over 3 years at my current job, and I was on career auto-pilot.

My daughter was born that May. My wife and I weren’t ready for being first time parents right at the height of the COVID pandemic. We struggled to find our stride balancing parenthood and work and everything else in between.

It reached a point where we decided that we needed to make drastic changes. My wife would leave her job to focus on being the mom she wanted to be without the stress of work getting in the way.

My goal at that time became to make up for my wife’s lost income, so that she could comfortably focus on being a mom without feeling guilty for giving up her steady income.

I spoke to every recruiter that reached out to me. I applied to jobs at all the big tech companies and tons of mid-sized companies for senior roles. I took on big projects at work to give myself more experiences to talk about during interviews, and to build my confidence. I worked late hours. Everything I did during that time was tied directly to that goal.

There were many low points, lots of failed interviews, a job offer that was lower than I wanted, then a job offer that was better but still not where I wanted to be. But I took the new job and was hyper-focused on bringing everything I could to that job. I had failed enough big tech interviews to know exactly what I needed to improve on to get an offer, so I spent my time at the new job working tirelessly to build that missing experience.

Later in the book I’ll go into more detail on the approaches I took to prepare for the Amazon interview that ultimately landed the job that got me to reach my goal. But that was a year and a half long journey that wouldn’t have been possible without that goal motivating me for the long term.

What even is motivation?

Motivation can be defined as the driving force that encourages individuals or groups to reach their goals. It is the internal or external stimulus that stimulates interest, desire, and the willingness to put in the effort to achieve a particular outcome or maintain a particular behavior.
In psychology, motivation is often classified into two categories:
Intrinsic motivation, which refers to doing something because it is inherently satisfying or pleasurable, or because it fulfills a personal interest or passion.
Extrinsic motivation, which refers to doing something in order to obtain some external reward or avoid a negative consequence.

Back to the higher order question: How do I motivate myself to pursue (some goal/action) rather than (some other goal/action)?

Let's come up with an example:

How do I motivate myself to pursue learning software development rather than playing video games?

How do I motivate myself to pursue financial freedom rather than happiness?

How do I motivate myself to pursue writing this book rather than spending my free time dissociating on social media?

What I realized in coming up with that example was that both goals or actions can be productive and worthy of striving for. But there is a missing variable here, and that has to do with the current state. In the above example, maybe I'm happy with my current state, but it's beneficial for me to focus more on a new goal or action, even if that means temporarily lowering my current state.

So there needs to be some stimulus that pushes me toward an immediate state that, if I'm focusing purely on the present, maybe seems like a downgrade, but if I expand my focus to a longer-term view, will be an upgrade. So that's another variable: time.

If we are only looking at let's say the next 5 minutes, the motivation will need to be more extreme to get me to take action toward goal A over goal B. And let's call goal B my current goal, and goal A the new goal.

But if we expand the timeframe to the next year, then the motivation that it will take for me to pursue the new goal will be less extreme than the previous example of 5 minutes.

So we've added 2 new variables to this question: How do I motivate myself to pursue (some goal) rather than (some other goal)?
