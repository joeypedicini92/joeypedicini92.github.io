Impostor Syndrome

Impostor Syndrome is something I still struggle with, but I’m much more comfortable and confident than when I started my career 10 years ago.

When you get a ticket (task), and you read it, and your first thought is “I don’t know what any of this means and have no idea where to even begin”, what feelings come up?

If you are anything like me, I’m sure you feel nervous, anxious, “do I deserve this job?”.

A past mentee of mine sent me this message about her first week on a new job. I’d like to share her reflection and questions, and my response:

A past client’s experience with impostor syndrome
I feel like this week I understood nothing. I am very lost, and my imposter syndrome is at an all-time high. I always had a great fear of approaching new technologies and very backend-oriented tasks, not because I don't want to study them, as I love studying new things, but because I feel like I would have never been capable of using them in a job. I felt like I had to learn them on my own before approaching them in an actual work environment. However, the senior IT manager, who is also my mentor, was pretty confident I would learn if I had passion. But these weeks really feel like I never actually learned how to code in my entire life. My mentor told me they are more software developers than web developers and that, as they aren't a software company, they just find ways to improve their code as they can. They don't have all the fancy documentation or ticket management systems like Jira. So, I guess what I am asking is, how should I approach this? My mentor really wants me to be a part of the team but is confused about how to insert me into the project. He talks a lot about technical things I am not familiar with and about what the company does, so other technicalities not even related to coding. Consequently, I don't have any clear idea of what I actually need to implement and how. I did talk about this with him, and he does seem to want to help, but he really struggles with explaining and falls into technical terms again by showing me many things all at once. I end up seeing random things with Java and Eclipse, and they feel like little random pieces of a puzzle I have no idea about, and I forget what I even saw the day after. So, I wonder if there is some problem on my part, if the feeling is normal, and things will make sense after a while, or if I need to adjust things with my mentor to find ways to work better with him. However, I don't want to be arrogant about it, as I have no idea how we can structure things in a way that makes sense for me.

Thank you for any advice on this!

Great questions and reflection on how you are feeling, that's a great foundation to improving the situation for yourself. Let's start with how you can work better with your mentor. Some ideas that come to mind:

Pair programming

Take turns leading and working through tasks. I do this a lot with junior engineers on my team and they all find it very beneficial to watch me code. And I find it beneficial to watch them and see what problems they are running into.

Record your meetings if possible

It helps to go back on your own time at your own pace to digest information.

Work with your mentor to find smaller more achievable tasks

Tasks that you can work on to build your confidence and understanding.

You mentioned they don't use Jira or any ticketing system, how do they assign tasks then? Impostor syndrome is very normal when starting a new job, especially when the new job is with all new technologies. First step is to acknowledge how you're feeling which you have done already, great! Next step is to accept that that is perfectly normal and ok, and very likely that it is only you feeling that way about yourself and none of your team perceives you that way.

Set up a weekly or bi-weekly check-in call

doesn't have to be long, but just get feedback as often as you can. What you will hopefully find is that you will get better feedback than what you imagine you would get.

Keep a journal or notebook

Write down questions that come to mind, take notes, keep track of the things you learn each day, write about how you are feeling. Then each week, maybe even during your check-in call or right before it, look back on the past week or two of notes and reflect on what you learned and how you felt and how those things evolve over time. When you are feeling particularly anxious, that's a good moment to reflect and do some writing. Or take a quick break and do some grounding exercise, like going for a walk, stretching, breathing exercises. And it's ok to take time off throughout the day, stay connected with yourself and when you need rest.

Your goal right now is not to be an expert on this codebase. Your goal is to survive this initial phase of feeling overwhelmed. Baby steps. Make a little progress each day and it'll compound over time. Hopefully that helps! Happy to dive deeper into anything or share personal anecdotes if you want. But I know you got this!

Journaling Prompts
  1. Reflect on recent accomplishments: List three things you have achieved recently, no matter how small. Acknowledge your efforts and the positive outcomes.
  1. Identify your strengths: Write down your top three strengths and think about how they have contributed to your success. Embrace and celebrate these qualities.
  1. Track positive feedback: Record any positive feedback or compliments you receive. Revisit these notes when you're feeling self-doubt.
  1. Challenge negative thoughts: Write down any negative thoughts or self-doubts you're experiencing. Then, counteract each one with evidence of your skills, achievements, or positive qualities.
  1. Set realistic goals: Outline short-term and long-term goals for yourself. Break them down into manageable steps, and celebrate your progress along the way.
  1. Recognize the learning process: Embrace the fact that everyone is constantly learning and growing. Journal about the lessons you've learned from challenges and mistakes.
  1. Compare yourself to your past self: Reflect on how much you've grown and improved over time. Focus on personal development rather than comparing yourself to others.
  1. Explore the root of your feelings: Dig deeper into the origins of your impostor syndrome. Were there specific experiences or influences that contributed to these feelings? Write about them.
  1. Practice self-compassion: Write a letter to yourself expressing kindness and understanding. Treat yourself with the same empathy you would offer to a friend facing similar struggles.
  1. Visualize success: Imagine yourself achieving your goals and feeling confident in your abilities. Visualizing success can help shift your mindset.
